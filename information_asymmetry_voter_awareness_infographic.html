2 · Asymmetry

The powerful side gets a dashboard. The voter gets a feed.

Information asymmetry means the actors trying to influence votes often know more about timing, targeting, persuasion, and weak spots than the average voter can realistically check.

Targeting advantage

Campaigns, advocacy groups, and platforms can segment audiences, test frames, and repeat tailored messages. Voters usually see only their own slice.

6 · Democracy consequences

Less local information can mean more cue-based voting.

Academic research links the decline of local news to reduced citizen engagement, lower local political knowledge, weaker competition, and more nationalized political behavior.

Why that changes accountability

When the local watchdog disappears, the cost of monitoring officials rises. Voters can still care deeply, but they may have less verified information about who did what, who benefited, and who should be held responsible.

What breaks the cycle

The antidote is not “more content.” It is better signal.

The fixes are structural: make reliable local information easier to find, make persuasion more transparent, and reduce the verification burden on individual voters.

Rebuild local reporting

Public meetings, budgets, courts, candidate records, and service outcomes need routine coverage.

Make sources legible

Clear ownership, funding, AI use, corrections, and ad disclosures help voters evaluate claims faster.

Prioritize issue explainers

Side-by-side policy comparisons and local impact explainers reduce reliance on vibes and slogans.

Expose ranking incentives

Platform transparency can reveal when outrage, novelty, or ad revenue outranks public relevance.

Bottom line

When the information environment becomes asymmetric, overloaded, and low-trust, voter awareness becomes expensive. The actors who can pay to shape attention gain power — not because voters are careless, but because democratic attention has become a scarce resource.
